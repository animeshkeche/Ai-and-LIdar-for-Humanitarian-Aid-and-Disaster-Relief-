Abstract. Quick, precise awareness of what’s happening on the ground like spotting smoke curling over a flooded 
street makes all the difference in a fast, effective disaster response. Traditional disaster assessments that rely on 
manual surveys, satellite images, and drone cameras often respond too slowly, capture limited detail, and struggle 
when visibility drops like trying to map a flooded street through thick fog. This paper introduces an AI-powered 
LiDAR framework that delivers high-resolution damage assessments and guides decisions after disasters, 
pinpointing cracks and debris with striking detail. The proposed method uses deep learning to process 3D LiDAR 
point clouds, automatically spotting cracks or breaks, ranking how severe the damage is, and marking the spots that 
rescuers should reach first. Tests on both benchmark and simulated datasets show that our method delivers higher 
accuracy and faster results than traditional disaster assessment techniques, pinpointing damage almost instantly on a 
live map. The results show that the proposed framework sharply boosts situational awareness, helping teams make 
quick, well-informed decisions when chaos hits a map glowing on a screen can mean the difference between delay 
and rescue.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Earthquakes, floods, cyclones, landslides, and wildfires keep striking, taking lives, wrecking roads and 
homes, and shaking economies across the world. Over the past few decades, these disasters have struck 
harder and more often, pushing humanitarian groups and emergency teams to rush in with fast, effective 
relief sometimes before the dust even settles [1, 2]. Humanitarian disaster response succeeds when teams 
quickly understand what’s happening on the ground, gauge the extent of the damage like flooded roads 
or collapsed roofs and get scarce supplies where they’re needed most. 
Most traditional disaster assessments lean on hands-on field surveys, satellite maps streaked with cloud 
cover, and reports gathered from the ground. Manual surveys may provide rich detail, but they’re slow, 
exhausting work and in unstable post-disaster zones, even stepping over cracked concrete can be 
dangerous [1]. Satellite imagery is now widely used to map large-scale damage, but it often falls short 
clouds drift across the lens, updates come slowly, and fine cracks in collapsed roofs slip past its view [3, 
4]. These limits often slow down decisions in the crucial first hours after disaster hits, when acting fast 
sometimes within minutes can mean the difference between life and death. 
Drones fitted with optical cameras now serve as a practical alternative to satellite monitoring, delivering 
sharper, close-up imagery and collecting data in a fraction of the time [8]. Even with all their benefits, 
camera-based UAVs still struggle when the light shifts, smoke thickens, or dust coats the air and debris 
or fallen beams can easily block their view. Plus, flat two-dimensional images don’t capture depth, so 
they fall short when it comes to judging real structural damage or subtle ground shifts after a disaster like 
when a cracked bridge shadow hides the gap beneath it.

LiDAR, short for Light Detection and Ranging, has rapidly become a standout sensing tool, especially 
for disaster assessment its quick laser pulses can trace 
flood damage with crisp precision, down to the curve of a washed-out road. LiDAR maps terrain and 
buildings in crisp 3D detail, cutting through smoke, dust, or even the dark like a beam slicing fog. Fixed 
to drones or rolling ground rigs, LiDAR sensors swiftly collect dense point [21]. cloud data that reveal 
changes in terrain, twisted beams, and bits of debris catching sharp flashes of sunlight. Previous studies 
found LiDAR data highly effective for identifying collapsed buildings, locating blocked roads, and 
helping crews chart disaster zones for example, tracing rubble-strewn streets after an earthquake [22, 
23]. 
Still, raw LiDAR point‑cloud data packs millions of tiny points like silver dust outlining every ridge and 
it demands serious computing muscle to sift through manually. This momentum’s driven researchers to 
fuse AI with LiDAR scans, streamlining how damage gets detected and analyzed almost like following 
hairline cracks along a scanned bridge deck. Deep learning models especially convolutional neural 
networks (CNNs) have shown remarkable skill in spotting distinctive patterns in LiDAR point clouds, 
such as the sharp edges of buildings or the rough silhouettes of trees, to support classification and 
segmentation tasks [24,25]. This kind of integration instantly gauges how bad the damage is like 
noticing a crack spidering across glass and helps teams size up the situation far faster than any manual 
check or rigid set of rules. 
When AI steps into humanitarian work, it stirs tough questions moral and logistical about how people 
and machines can push forward side by side through the grit, the rush, and the aching pulse of need. A 
wealth of studies stress that AI in disaster response has to remain open and accountable, backed by 
strong data protection and guided by people like a small crew kneeling beside a cracked sensor, brushing 
off the dust to test it after an earthquake [9,13,16]. AI systems build trust and behave responsibly when 
they follow humanitarian principles and respect human rights like a compass keeping a ship steady as 
cold spray stings the deck. 
Even with major advances in AI and LiDAR for disaster assessment, many systems still feel pieced 
together slow to process data in real time and poorly linked to the decision tools that guide humanitarian 
teams racing through dust and debris. Many methods still treat AI-driven image analysis and LiDAR 
mapping as separate tracks, missing a single system that fuses sharp 3D sensing with smart interpretation 
and real-world support [14,15]. This shows a clear gap in research on building integrated, AI-powered 
LiDAR systems built for quick damage checks and faster humanitarian decisions like mapping collapsed 
roofs moments after a storm. 
This study tackles that gap by introducing an AI-powered, LiDAR-based system that quickly gauges 
damage and speeds up humanitarian disaster reading the wreckage patterns left after a storm in seconds. 
The approach uses 3D LiDAR point-cloud data and deep learning to spot structural damage, gauge how 
severe it is, and flag the areas that most need rescue and relief like the twisted beams at a collapsed 
bridge. By sharpening situational awareness faster, clearer, more precise the framework helps teams 
make quicker calls and deliver lifesaving aid when it matters most, even amid the dust and noise of a 
disaster zone.

3.2 Experimental Setup 
The experimental evaluation was conducted in controlled environment using standard computational 
resources. The AI-LiDAR framework in a controlled environment. This way, everything stayed fair and 
we could repeat the process without any surprises. 
We split the dataset—70% for training, 30% for testing. Stratified sampling kept all the different damage 
types in both sets, so nothing got left out. The model got trained on the bigger chunk, then we tested it 
with fresh data to see if it could handle brand new cases. 
For classification, we used a Convolutional Neural Network. It ran through 50 training epochs with the 
Adam optimizer and a learning rate of 0.001. If the validation loss stopped getting better, we cut training 
short to avoid overfitting and save some time. 
The dataset was divided into training (70%) and testing (30%) sets using stratified sampling., everything 
was done in Python. We handled LiDAR data preprocessing with Open3D and NumPy, and kept the 
whole setup on a regular lab machine. No fancy gear—just a normal computer anyone with access could 
use. 
To make sure our findings held up, we ran each experiment a few times. We tracked accuracy, precision, 
recall, and F1-score to cover all the main performance  
metrics. 
And then, we took it one step further. We compared our model against baselines like satellite data and 
UAV camera assessments, just to see how the AI-LiDAR system measured up in real disaster 
scenarios.

3.3 Evaluation Metrics 
We track the framework’s performance with metrics for accuracy, response time, and a few others that 
show how smoothly it runs once the data rushes through like water in a clear glass tube. These metrics 
often steer how we assess and classify disasters, giving a clear, full view of a system’s performance like 
tracing every fracture in a broken wall to feel where it’s weakest. 
Accuracy shows how often the system correctly identifies damage for example, catching the glint of a 
cracked windshield in the sun. 
Precision shows how reliable those predicted damage classes are it’s like pausing to make sure each 
cracked beam gets tagged correctly before you step away. 
Recall measures how sharply the system detects damaged spots for example, a thin crack cutting across 
a strip of rusted metal. 
The F1-score balances precision and recall, a scale so steady that even a grain of dust barely tips it. 
Response Time tracks how fast the system delivers damage assessments like a report that pops up the 
instant a photo finishes loading. 
Together, these metrics measure the analysis’s accuracy and show how it holds up when time runs short 
like hauling crates of medicine through knee-deep water on a frantic relief run.

CONCLUSION 
This paper presents an AI-driven LiDAR system that rapidly gauges damage to aid disaster relief, tracing 
collapsed rooftops and waterlogged streets within minutes. The proposed method blends crisp, 3-D LiDAR 
scans with deep-learning tools to automatically detect structural damage, measure its severity, and guide 
crews in making sound decisions as they inspect a cracked wall or tilted beam after a disaster. 
Tests on standard LiDAR datasets and in simulated disaster zones showed our framework outperformed 
traditional tools, surpassing both satellite imagery and camera-based UAV methods a clear edge, like 
spotting fine cracks in rubble others missed. The results showed clearer classification, stronger precision and 
recall, and faster reaction times a solid sign that combining LiDAR’s crisp 3D view with AI-powered 
analysis heightens situational awareness, like seeing every ridge on a moving surface in real time. 
Its modular design makes the system quick to scale and ready for anything— from salt-stung coastal floods 
to wildfires racing through dry mountain pines, whatever the terrain. By reducing manual interpretation and 
letting automation spot damage—say, a collapsed roof glinting in satellite photos— the framework could 
make humanitarian disaster response faster and more reliable. The results look promising, but the study tests 
performance only offline, using benchmark data and a handful of simulated runs that hum quietly on a lab 
machine.
